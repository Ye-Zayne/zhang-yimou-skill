# 张艺谋导演方法论参考

Use this file when converting short-drama scripts into Zhang Yimou-inspired cinematic language. Treat all items as craft principles, not a license to copy protected scenes.

## 风格矩阵

### 1. Color Is Dramaturgy

Color must answer: who has power, who is trapped, what truth is hidden, what emotion is rising?

- Red: vitality, desire, blood, wedding, shame, rebellion, ritual control. Use carefully; red can liberate or imprison.
- Yellow/earth: land, poverty, grain, old order, endurance, fate.
- Black/gold: imperial order, institutional power, wealth, corruption, oppressive ceremony.
- White/snow/ash: erasure, secrecy, mourning, surveillance, cold moral test.
- Green/bamboo/jade: temptation, ambush, youth, false calm.
- Ink gray: ambiguity, doubles, tactical concealment, suppressed violence.

Short-drama translation:

- Assign one color to each force, not each character randomly.
- Use the color to change across beats: clean red -> stained red, bright gold -> sickly gold, white snow -> gray footprints.
- In low-budget scenes, use small objects: scarf, receipt stamp, plastic basin, phone case, thermos, school ribbon, clinic curtain.

### 2. Ritual Reveals Control

A ritual is any repeated social action with rules:

- wedding toast, family meal, school roll call, factory inspection, village meeting, funeral queue, court mediation, hospital payment window, company morning meeting, live-stream order countdown.

Make the ritual visually legible:

- Who sits at the center?
- Who stands?
- Who pours tea or wine?
- Who is forced to wait?
- Who repeats the line everyone expects?
- Who breaks rhythm first?

Scene move:

1. Establish the ritual.
2. Repeat it enough for the audience to learn the rule.
3. Insert one wrong gesture.
4. Let everyone react before anyone explains.

### 3. Crowd Vs Individual

Zhang-style pressure often comes from one person facing an organized shape.

Use patterns:

- line: bureaucracy, judgment, waiting.
- circle: accusation, spectacle, public shame.
- wall: family/company/institutional refusal.
- procession: fate already moving.
- empty center: absent authority or missing truth.

For vertical 9:16:

- Replace big crowd with compressed arrangements: three elders in a row, coworkers seen through glass, neighbors stacked on stairwell landings, phones forming a ring around the subject.
- Use foreground obstruction: door frame, curtain, lattice, window bars, queue rope, elevator doors.

### 4. Space Is Power

Design space before dialogue.

- Courtyard: hierarchy, surveillance, inherited rules.
- Alley: rumor, pursuit, social compression.
- Banquet hall: face, debt, family order, public humiliation.
- Schoolyard: fairness, childhood, moral accounting.
- Factory/workshop: labor rhythm, class pressure, mechanical fate.
- Kitchen: female labor, care, resentment, hidden truth.
- Office/window counter: procedure over emotion.
- Snow/rain/night street: exposed conscience, secrecy, irreversible choice.

Rule: the protagonist's movement through space should mirror their change in power.

### 5. Object As Verdict

Choose one recurring object and make it change meaning.

Good object traits:

- visible in close-up;
- cheap enough for short-drama production;
- culturally legible;
- can be handled, stained, broken, hidden, or passed from person to person.

Examples:

- red envelope, bowl, seal, medical bill, phone recording, wedding shoe, schoolbag, paper lantern, contract, key, urn, blood-stained cloth, old photo, account book.

Object arc:

1. Appears ordinary.
2. Is claimed by power.
3. Is mishandled or concealed.
4. Returns as evidence or emotional verdict.

### 6. Women Under Pressure

Do not turn women into passive suffering icons. Give them a strategy.

Strong pattern:

- She cannot directly challenge the system, so she learns its rules better than the people who control it.
- Her body is framed by the institution, but her small actions disturb the ritual.
- Her silence is tactical, not empty.

Weak pattern:

- She only cries, waits, or exists as visual beauty.
- Her pain motivates a male protagonist without her own decision.

### 7. Dialogue And Performance

Dialogue should be plain, indirect, and loaded by context.

Prefer:

- "你把碗放回去。"
- "今天人齐，不说不合适。"
- "章盖了，事就算完了。"
- "你看见的是门，我看见的是路。"

Avoid:

- long philosophical monologues;
- explaining visual metaphors;
- everyone speaking in slogans;
- modern internet punchlines in solemn scenes unless doing comedy-suspense.

Performance:

- Hold eye contact one beat too long.
- Let a hand complete what the mouth refuses.
- Use stillness before violence or truth.
- Let the crowd react with silence, not chatter.

### 8. Sound System

Use sound as pressure:

- drumbeat, suona, opera line, footsteps in corridor, stamp hitting paper, boiling water, wind through cloth, school bell, loudspeaker notice, chopsticks, camera shutter, phone vibration.

Short-drama rule:

- Give each episode one repeatable sound hook.
- Cut sound to silence before a reveal.
- Use offscreen crowd sound to expand production scale cheaply.

### 9. Suspense And Reversal

For recent Zhang-style type-film energy:

- Put characters in a sealed time window: one night, one banquet, one courtyard, one office floor, one inspection, one family ceremony.
- Make every character carry a hidden role.
- Use comedy or procedural absurdity to keep tempo, then turn it into moral consequence.
- Let the final reversal reassign guilt, not merely reveal information.

Reversal ladder:

1. What the audience thinks happened.
2. What the institution says happened.
3. What the family needs to be true.
4. What the object proves.
5. What the protagonist chooses to do with the truth.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Color cosplay**: red clothes everywhere with no story logic.
- **Empty spectacle**: big crowd or ceremony that does not change the protagonist.
- **Museum China**: props feel like tourist decoration rather than lived-in life.
- **Overwritten suffering**: characters explain pain that the image could show.
- **Flattened rurality**: countryside equals backwardness; avoid this.
- **Patriarchal nostalgia**: oppressive customs are shown as beautiful without critique.
- **No production logic**: scenes demand impossible extras, locations, or stunts for a short-drama budget.

## Quality Gate

Before final output, check:

- Can the story be understood from five still frames?
- Does the first image hook contain conflict?
- Does every color/object choice have narrative function?
- Is there at least one ritual or repeated action that breaks?
- Does the protagonist make a choice, not merely suffer?
- Is the ending an image, not only a line?
- Can the scene be shot in the user's stated budget and format?
- For 9:16, are faces, hands, props, and subtitles readable on a phone?
