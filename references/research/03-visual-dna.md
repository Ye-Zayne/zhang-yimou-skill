# 03 · 影像表达 DNA

Use this file when writing shot lists, storyboard prompts, image prompts, Seedance prompts, or director notes.

## Color Grammar

Color answers four questions:

1. Who owns the space?
2. Who is being watched?
3. What truth is hidden?
4. What emotion is about to break?

Recommended color functions:

| Color | Function | Low-budget objects |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Red | desire, wedding, blood, shame, rebellion, ritual control | envelope, curtain, scarf, stamp ink, basin, ribbon |
| Yellow/earth | land, poverty, endurance, old order | wall, grain bag, work gloves, tungsten bulb |
| Black/gold | wealth, authority, corruption, imperial order | suit, plaque, banquet chair, elevator interior |
| White/snow/ash | erasure, secrecy, mourning, cold moral test | bedsheet, hospital curtain, paper, smoke, snow |
| Green/jade | temptation, false calm, youth, ambush | plant, bracelet, office glass, school uniform detail |
| Ink gray | ambiguity, double identity, tactical concealment | umbrella, shadow, wet ground, concrete corridor |

Rule: one scene should have a dominant color and a conflict color. More than that usually becomes decorative noise.

## Composition Grammar

Use geometry to say power:

- Center vs edge: power vs exclusion.
- High vs low: judgment vs submission.
- Door inside door: trapped choices.
- Glass reflection: hidden identity or institutional separation.
- Circle: public accusation.
- Line: bureaucracy, waiting, rank.
- Empty chair: absent authority or missing truth.
- Symmetry: order, ceremony, coercion.
- Broken symmetry: rebellion, confession, accident.

## Camera Grammar

| Need | Camera choice |
| --- | --- |
| Establish ritual | fixed wide or medium-wide |
| Increase pressure | slow push-in |
| Follow small-person struggle | handheld or restrained follow |
| Reveal object evidence | close-up with short hold |
| Show power reversal | move subject from edge to center |
| Phone vertical readability | close-up, medium close, foreground object, shallow blocking |

Avoid camera moves without dramatic function. A move should change information, pressure, or power.

## Blocking Grammar

Design bodies before lines:

1. Draw the power center.
2. Put the protagonist away from it.
3. Place a threshold they cannot cross.
4. Put the object near the authority.
5. Let one action move the object or protagonist into the center.

Micro-crowd options:

- Three elders in a row = family court.
- Four coworkers behind glass = company wall.
- Neighbors stacked on stairs = village gossip.
- Phones raised in circle = digital mob.
- Two people at doorway, one inside = exclusion.

## Sound Grammar

Sound is pressure, not background:

- Repeated sound: rules and routine.
- Offscreen sound: larger society outside frame.
- Sudden silence: moral reveal.
- Mechanical sound: procedure over emotion.
- Folk/percussive sound: ceremony and bodily tension.

Useful motifs:

- stamp hitting paper;
- chopsticks against porcelain;
- boiling kettle;
- school bell;
- elevator ding;
- phone vibration;
- loudspeaker announcement;
- footsteps in corridor;
- suona/drum fragment;
- rain on plastic awning.

## Performance Grammar

Prefer restrained pressure:

- Hold eye contact one beat too long.
- Let a hand stop before touching the object.
- Let someone sit while everyone else stands.
- Let the protagonist swallow a line and act instead.
- Delay tears until after the decisive gesture.

## Storyboard Prompt Formula

```text
black and white pencil storyboard frame, vertical 9:16, [shot size], [location], [character continuity], [blocking], [main object], [camera angle], [emotion], clean line art, low detail, no text, no subtitles, no watermark
```

## Video Prompt Formula

```text
9:16 cinematic short-drama shot, [duration], [location], [character continuity], [action], [camera movement], [color system], [sound/mood], [object motif], realistic production design, no text, no watermark
```
